First IoT DIY

26.03.2018




A few months ago I came across an amazing variety of the Internet of Things (IoT) DIY possibilities that an amateur with no electric or IT education can do these days. There are plenty of open source services, which allow you to send data, store it and visualize it, not mentioning the simple load controls like relays. But it actually took me a while to realize that. When I finally did, I run to order a bunch of "made in China" microcontrollers and sensors to set a WiFi weather monitoring station in may room (the first thing that a newby like me would do). They took a month to come and this is what came out of all of that.

A thing like weather monitoring station could make use in lots of projects, but for me it was more of a challenge rather than a wish to know the air quality in my own room, and of course I wanted it to work on WiFi. In addition, my Master's thesis expreriment involved growing trees plantlets in a growth chamber. While a chamber has an LCD monitor, where all the indoor conditions are shown, it does not allow to store the data, and one cannot know the exact conditions inside the chamber without actually being at the university and looking at the display. Such device could make a hell lot of use during that thesis experiment. At this point the choice of a microcontroller was already obvious. I decieded to use ESP8266 by Node-MCU, because it has all the features of an Arduino Nano/Mini/Uno plus an integrated WiFi module, and it is very cheap. And more importantly, the most of the libraries that are out there for Arduino are fully compartible with ESP8266 and the board itself can be programmed in an Arduino IDE.

There many servises to process the data if you are an amateur or do not want to bother yourself with setting up your own server. My choice fell on Blynk and ThingSpeak, two open source cloud platforms for IoT projects that easily work together. Blynk is a mobile app, which works with the most popular microcontrollers like arduino, ESP32/8266 and also Raspberry Pi. And ThingSpeak is the similar platform but working in the internet browser.

There is also a nice boquette of sensors to chose from. I took BME280 because it measures air temperature, humidity and pressure simultaneously. The sensor has an analog-to-digital converter (ADC), and works by I2C bus, so the connection is also an easy task. It can work with 3.3V of ESP8266 output.
